# Synthesis Patterns Reference

## Thematic Analysis

Group findings by theme, not by paper. Each theme represents a claim or concept supported by multiple sources.

### Structure Per Theme

1. **Opening statement** — the claim or finding in plain language
2. **Supporting evidence** — cite 2-3 studies that support the claim
3. **Contradictory evidence** — cite any studies that challenge the claim (if they exist)
4. **Confidence tag** — rate the strength of evidence

### Example

> Autonomy support in the workplace is consistently associated with higher intrinsic motivation (Deci & Ryan, 2017; Gagné et al., 2015). A meta-analysis of 99 studies confirmed this relationship across cultures (Van den Broeck et al., 2016). However, the effect is moderated by job complexity, with weaker associations in highly structured roles (Humphrey et al., 2007).
> **Confidence:** HIGH

## Confidence Tagging Criteria

| Tag | Criteria |
|-----|----------|
| **HIGH** | Multiple replicated studies, meta-analyses, systematic reviews, consistent findings across contexts |
| **MEDIUM** | Single well-designed study, limited replication, or findings from a narrow population/context |
| **LOW** | Theoretical proposals without empirical testing, anecdotal evidence, conflicting findings with no resolution, pilot studies, small samples |

### Rules

- Default to MEDIUM when uncertain — it is the honest middle ground
- Never assign HIGH to a single study, regardless of journal prestige
- Conflicting evidence does not automatically mean LOW — if both sides are well-supported, note the disagreement and tag MEDIUM
- Preprints (arXiv) without peer review cap at MEDIUM unless replicated

## Handling Contradictory Evidence

Do not suppress contradictions. Present them explicitly:

1. State the dominant finding
2. Note the contradictory evidence with citation
3. If possible, explain the discrepancy (methodology, population, context)
4. Let the confidence tag reflect the uncertainty

## Structuring a Literature Review

### For Quick Scans (5-10 sources)

- 2-3 themes, 1 paragraph each
- Summary table of papers reviewed
- Reference list

### For Comprehensive Reviews (15+ sources)

- 4-6 themes, 2-3 paragraphs each
- Identification of gaps in the literature
- Summary table of papers reviewed
- Reference list

## Avoiding Common Errors

- Do not cite papers you have not retrieved metadata for — verify author, year, and title
- Do not present correlation as causation unless the study design supports it
- Do not generalise findings beyond the population studied
- Do not treat citation count as a proxy for quality — it reflects visibility, not rigour
- State explicitly when evidence is limited rather than overstating confidence
